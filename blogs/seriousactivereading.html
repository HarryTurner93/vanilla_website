Seriously Active Reading

20th December 2020

Introduction

What is this article all about and why should you care? Active reading (in contrast with passive reading) is the process of trying to actively remember what we read by engaging with the material as we read it. More often than not that means taking notes, pausing regularly to reflect and connect the ideas with our other experiences, and following up with the information afterward using techniques like spaced repetition. The advantage of doing this is obvious, we learn more and remember more of it, who doesn't want that!

Unfortunately, active reading takes a lot of effort, and we simply can't apply it to everything we read, it'd be too exhausting. But there are certainly occasions where we do want to really absorb and assimilate the material, and it's nice to have this "turbo mode" that we can switch on and off as and when we need it. Ideally, we want our turbo mode to be as effective and efficient as possible so that we're maximising the amount of value we get out of reading a particular piece. These methods are sometimes called Reading Strategies, or Reading Systems.

This article is a tutorial in my system for extracting as much value as possible from the material, remembering it, and then applying it in my life. I wanted to share it here as I've been asked about it a few times and it's useful to have somewhere to direct people to. This system is a variation of other reading strategies out there that I have personalised and made my own, I hope you find some of the ideas useful. Here's a quick summary of how my system works, then I'll go through each part in more detail with an example case study.

My Reading System

First I decide whether or not the thing I'm reading or watching is something I really want to learn and remember, in other words, I decide whether or not to turn on turbo-mode. If I do, then I specifically look out for and extract mental models, principles, and tools. I add these to three growing lists respectively, that I store in Evernote. Finally, I have adopted the habit of regularly reviewing those lists (which is my version of spaced repetition), to help me remember and embed those ideas into everyday life. That's it.

Now I'll go through each of those pieces in turn.

Mental Models

Mental models are concepts or ideas about how something works, they're how we understand the world. The real world is far too complex for us to hold all the tiny details in our minds, so we use models to simplify that complexity, whether we realise it or not. Let me give you a few examples.

The Three Bucket Model. This is a mental model for thinking about product management in software projects. It says that product features can be grouped into three categories. The first is Game Changers which are features that make people buy your product, they're new, novel, and solve a frustrating problem. The second category of features is Show-Stoppers, which won't make people buy your product, but will stop them from buying it if they're not there. The third category of features is Distractors which are irrelevant and don't affect a buy decision. The reality is obviously more nuanced than this, but as a simplifying model, this way of thinking is surprisingly effective and helps me triage what features should be built first. Note that it doesn't tell me what to do, it just helps me think more effectively about the problem. In a minute I'll cover principles which tell me what to do. If I combine a popular principle such as Double down on your strengths with the Three Bucket Model, it tells me to prioritise the features that make my product stand out at all costs, whilst patching the show-stoppers sufficiently enough that they don't stop sales.

Information Compression. This is a model for thinking about communication between two people. It says that an idea may exist in the head of Person A, but when they try to communicate that idea to person B it has to pass through a bottleneck which is human language, and all the nuance and complexity of the idea is lost, which often means person B ends up not fully understanding the idea and may end up solving the wrong problem. When we understand this, we can either make sure we're communicating more of our idea or ensure that we're communicating the most important parts of the idea, which is effectively a better information compression. The article is well worth a read if that idea sounds interesting to you.

Most mental models appear in a particular domain, the Three Bucket Model is targetted at software product management for example. But some models are more widely applicable, and if you can map the concept into different areas of your life then the model may be called a general model. For example, we could try and apply the Three Bucket model to cooking recipes, or even ourselves, in that case, we would consider what skills we had that make us stand out, those are the game-changers. What weaknesses will stop us from getting the jobs we want? Those are the show-stoppers. The model helps us understand that we need to patch our weaknesses sufficiently to prevent them from becoming show-stoppers, but after that, we should probably double down on our strengths if we want to stand out from the other applicants.

An in-depth explanation of mental models can be found here.

Principles

A principle is a term I give to the little rules of thumb that I've collected throughout my lifetime, the proverbial fatherly wisdom handed down through the generations. They are almost like rules but tend to be rather more abstract and general. You can think of them simply as bits of general-purpose advice. The goal of a principle is to guide decision making, particularly in deciding how to act and behave in various situations. Let me explain with a few examples.

Prioritise Foundational Skills. This is a principle for helping me decide what to learn because there's far too much to learn for one lifetime. This principle reminds me that programming frameworks and languages come and go but that mastering the underlying concepts and ideas beneath those frameworks is more enduring. No doubt you've heard similar advice before, this is my way of encoding and remembering it. Notice how it's more of a directive, rather than a model for thinking about the world. This is the main difference between mental models and principles.

Affordable Loss Principle. I discovered this one whilst reading the article, What Makes Entrepreneurs Entrepreneurial? It dictates that a good way of testing your product, or idea, is to simply try it out, and do so as cheaply as possible. For example, just go and try to sell your product to see if it sells, post your blog post to see if people like it. You'll learn a lot more this way. The idea is that working hard to make everything perfect and putting all the pieces in place before you "go live", is likely not as effective as simply going for it and adapting as you go. Note that this works well for entrepreneurs, probably less well for something like aircraft design, but there is a skill in knowing when to apply which principles. Also, note that I call it the Affordable Loss Principle because that's how it's known in the literature, the principle itself says to find ways to accomplish what you want to do with the minimal possible expenditure of effort, which is a directive, so it does feel like a principle rather than a mental model under my definitions.

You can find lists and lists of principles all over the internet, usually from articles named things like "50 Lessons for life", or "The Best Advice My Dad Ever Gave Me". Although these are good places to start, I've found it more effective to build my list with those that are the most meaningful to me, it grows over time. The most valuable place I pick up new principles now is from reading blogs and articles from insightful writers.

You do have to be careful of the level at which you collect them, otherwise, you may end up with far too many. For example, "Wake up at 7 am" probably isn't a principle worth keeping in your list, however perhaps "Keep a consistent sleeping routine" is more appropriate, it depends on what's important and useful to you.

Tools

Tools are mental devices that you can use and deploy to achieve a particular result, just like you would a real tool. Examples include step-by-step procedures for acting in particular situations or particular diagrams you might draw to help you think about an idea. I'll give a few examples.

Dr ABC. This is a classic tool for remembering what to do when you come across someone in need of medical assistance. It's a mnemonic device that teaches you to first assess the Danger, then try to elicit a Response, then if no response, check for Airway, Breathing, and Circulation. I don't personally keep this tool in my list, but it's been drilled into me so many times as a kid that it's the first example that popped into my mind.

A Process for Writing Meeting Notes. Here is a tool that you might use more often, I came across the article a few months ago. It describes a process for capturing meeting notes that goes like this. During the meeting, don't take notes, pay absolute attention to what your partner is saying. Afterward, when you get home, start writing notes. Begin with a flow of consciousness and use environmental clues to jog your memory. When that dries up, start mind-mapping, that usually helps you remember a few more pieces. Next, draw a picture of the other person (seriously), the switch to a different medium and way of thinking jogs yet more memories. Next add a time-line of the conversation, that usually dislodges yet a few more, finally write it all up. I'll admit that I don't do this yet, it's a lot of effort, but I know it's there and I've captured it in my list of tools. It's something I may experiment with in the future.

Regular Review

I have always struggled with scheduling regular sessions for reviewing notes, but I've read time and again that if you want to remember it for good, then that's what you need to do. I believe that the mental models, principles, and tools I'm collecting are really valuable, and if I end up in one of those situations where they may be useful, I want them to come to mind quickly and automatically, I don't want to have to say, hang on a minute and get my phone out and start scrolling through the list to find the most relevant one.

So it's not good enough to simply have a list of them, I need to know them by heart, I need to live and breathe them, so if I'm going to use spaced repetiton on at least one thing, it might as well be these lists. So that's what I do, the only repetition I do is to go over those lists, I spend a least a little bit of time each day just reading those lists. Specifically, I glance at the name of the model or principle and then try and explain it to myself with an example, then check if I'm correct, and I try and use different examples each time. It surprised me how quickly they become internalised and I've started noticing in my conversations with people that the right ones are starting to pop up, and I'll say, ah yes, that's like this idea, or oh that's like this thing, we can think of this problem this way, etc. I'm starting to believe that this process works.

A Worked Example

To tie everything together, I want to take you through an example of how I used this process during a short training course on Problem Solving that I attended recently at work. The course was only an hour-long, which is about as much time as I can deeply concentrate for, and trust me, this process requires deep concentration. The course was split into four sections.

  • Introduction to Problem Solving

  • Working Through the Problem

  • Problem Solving Tools

  • Practice Problem Solving

As a side note, the nice thing about structured training courses like this one is that they're usually designed so that each section has one main idea or concept behind it. The course designer may not be consciously thinking about whether or not that idea is a principle or mental model or tool, but it doesn't matter, it makes it easy for me to pick up. The general format for this course was that each section was broken down into a set of short lessons, each a couple of minutes long. Each lesson usually made one major point, and then the rest of the lesson contained several examples elaborating that point.

My method therefore involved paying close attention during the first minute or so of the lesson to pick out the model or principle, and then filling in any additional useful commentary whilst watching that idea be applied in practice.

Disclaimer. I've been very careful not to share too much course material here, even though it's not a public course, I don't want to infringe on any copyright. My goal here is to show you how I watch such a course and extract valuable ideas from it, not to teach you problem solving. All the extracted mental models and principles I think are fair game to share, these are generic problem solving concepts. All specific examples I have provided are however my own and for illustration, I haven't copied anything concrete from the course itself.

Introduction to Problem Solving

The first section was broken into three parts. I'll go through each in turn.

  • The Importance of Problem Solving

  • Causes vs Consequences

  • Breaking Problems Down

The Importance of Problem Solving. The first lesson was a sales pitch on why I should take the rest of the course, it said that rushing off to solve problems without thinking usually leads to solving the wrong problem and wasting time, so it pays to think critically about the problem first. I know all this, it's why I'm doing the course in the first place, so nothing to extract here.

Causes vs Consequences. The second lesson dived straight into actionable advice, this immediately screamed Principles! The instructor made two points, you must ensure you understand the root cause of the problem, not the superficial one you may be starting with. Secondly, ensure you understand the consequences of taking a particular action. What sequence of events could unfold as a result of you implementing your solution. These points were explained in the first thirty seconds of the lesson, and then the remainder developed the ideas with several examples and more information. Perfect I thought, there are two problem solving principles here. 1. Evaluate the Root Cause, and 2. Consider the Consequences.

Breaking Problems Down The last lesson of the section followed the same pattern, the first thirty seconds just said break the problem down, but then the instructor followed it up with several examples. At first, it felt like another principle, but when they stepped through the example it felt more like a variation of Evaluate the Root Cause. Splitting your big problem in two was reflecting the fact that the problem was influenced by these two smaller causes. So I didn't add a new principle, I expanded the definition of Evaluate the Root Cause to include the need for breaking the problem down into pieces.

Working Through the Problem

Section 2 was made up of the following parts.

  • Understand the Problem

  • Ask the Question Behind the Question

  • Look at What was Done Previously

  • Consider Multiple Points of View

Understand the Problem. The instructor made one point here, you need to understand the problem before you go off and solve it, and the best way to do that is to write a problem statement. A quick google search shows a generic problem statement consists of the goal, boundaries, success criteria, assumptions, stakeholders, and timelines. There was a lot of useful stuff to unpack here. First, I extracted a new principle, 3. Understand the Problem. You could argue this is the same as Evaluate the Root Cause, but it felt different, to me, this one is about making sure you're quantifying what it means to solve your problem, not necessarily identifying the actual cause of the problem. I added a tool as well, the Problem Statement, because the way it was presented in the lesson felt like a step by step process. It was a checklist to follow. I created a tool called the Problem Statement that involves answering the questions.

  • What is the goal?

  • How do you know you've solved the problem? (Success Criteria)

  • What are your assumptions?

  • Who are the stakeholders?

  • By when does this need to be solved?

  • What is and is not in scope?

Ask the Question Behind the Question. In this lesson, the instructor started by describing the tendency for us to rush off and start solving a problem the moment a co-worker asks us something. Here's my example, imagine you get asked Can you dig out those reports on our last quarter sales projections for me please? Or something along those lines. How many of us just rush off and do it? Whereas if you ask them why they want those particular reports, you might find out that what they really need isn't the reports themselves but that they've been tasked with collecting all the sales reports into the same place to keep the business organised. But what they don't know is that you've already done this for all the reports in your department and therefore can save them a lot of time. In other words, you know something that they don't, which you could exploit to save everybody some effort if only you understood what their actual problem was.

Initially, I thought this might be a principle, but as I considered it, I realised it was a variation of Understand the Problem, where the real problem is in the mind of the questioner. But there was something else here I thought, I felt like I couldn't just brush over it, I'd be missing out on something insightful. I decided to create a mental model that I called Question Behind the Question, and it goes like this. It's the idea that when someone asks you something, there's all this stuff in their head that they haven't asked you, which is their actual problem, but they're asking you what they think will get them the solution (this is a variation of information compression). Can you see how this is a mental model? It's a simplifying assumption about how this particular piece of the world works, if you keep that model in mind next time someone asks you to help them solve a problem, then you will almost feel the nagging doubt that there's something else to this that they're not saying which will hopefully prompt you to ask them more about the underlying reasons for their question.

Look at What was Done Previously. In this lesson, the instructor made the point that you should go and look at previous projects to see if this has been done before and if so, ask yourself what is different now that wasn't the case back then. This was simply another principle and I captured it as principle number four, Don't Reinvent the Wheel.

Consider Multiple Points of View. In this lesson, the instructor made the point that you should consider the problem from the point of view of different people, such as the sales team, the CEO, the customer, or the engineering team. This will give you a new perspective on the problem. It made sense I thought, and simply captured it as another principle, Consider Different Points of View. I made a few notes as prompts: What if Constraints were removed? What does it look like from sales/ceo/engineering?

Problem Solving Tools

Section Three had the following sections.

  • Five Whys

  • Seven So-Whats

  • The 80-20 Rule

Five Whys. The Five Whys is very simple, it involves asking why five times to get to the root cause, what could be simpler than that. The instructor explained this in the first 30 seconds and then went through an example. I thought great, another tool, specifically a tool for getting to the root cause, so I connected it to the principle I captured earlier about Evaluating the Root Cause and made a note to use the Five Whys to help you do it. Note that the number five isn't to be taken literally, I assume it's there to give the tool a consistent name, and the Multiple Whys didn't sound quite as catchy. The spirit of the tool is to keep asking Why until you've got to the root cause.

Seven So Whats The Seven So Whats is identical to the Five Whys except that you ask What happens as a result? This time you do it seven times (presumably because five was already taken) either way you're assessing consequences, not causes. Again, the idea is very simple so I captured the tool in the first twenty seconds, the remainder of the ten-minute lesson was just expounding on the idea with an example. I connected the tool to the Evaluate the Consequences principle and made a note to use the tool to help.

Then something interesting happened.

I am a computer scientist by training, and I regularly think in terms of cause and effect (that's basically what debugging is). As I watched the instructor work through his examples of Five Whys and Seven So Whats, I watched him drill right down into a very specific issue. Let me show you what happened (swapping in a different example). The original issue was that the profit is declining. These are the five answers that came next.

  • Problem. Our profit is going down.

  • Why? Because our product sales are declining.

  • Why? Because the product lacks critical features.

  • Why? Because the development team didn't have time to implement them.

  • Why? Because they were too busy fixing bugs in other products.

  • Why? Because there aren't enough team members to support the workload.

  • Result. We need to hire more developers.

Hang on?? I thought to myself. Things are never that simple. Are you absolutely sure that the answers to each of those why questions is the one and only cause? There are probably lots of reasons for plummeting profits. Something didn't feel right. At that point, the mental models of graph theory and breadth vs depth-first search popped into my head. (These are mental models from the field of computer science).

I realised that when you start at the problem and ask why for the first time, there are multiple answers, and any one of them could be the cause. In reality, they probably all contribute slightly. You are in a tree, not a chain. If you ask why again, you move up another level in the tree, and because there is probably a high branching factor, you suddenly have a large number of possible root causes. If you ask why five times, you could end up in a situation where you have possibly hundreds of different root causes. How can we be so sure we've got the right one? Once I realised this, I knew Five Whys could be dangerous if not used properly, because the reality is that there is a tangled web of cause and effect leading to your original problem, and Five Whys is a depth-first search down one path of the tree (as is Seven So Whats).

Assumption made by Five Whys and Seven So-What's.

The Reality

The same applies to the consequences, it's a branching tree, not a chain. From this, I extracted a new mental model, The Cause and Effect Tree. The model says that cause and effect are complex, so be wary of pouring all of your efforts into a single root cause without being absolutely sure it is the right one!

The 80-20 Rule. The 80-20 rule is the idea that 80% of the outputs come from 20% of the inputs. This can be applied very widely, 80% of software bugs come from 20% of the features, 80% of the customer support desk effort comes from 20% of the customers, etc. Sometimes people get stuck on the 80/20 split, but it's just a power law, and it doesn't even have to add up to 100%. You can have 90% of your problems coming from 1% of the customers etc. I already had this captured in my lists, it's a widely known mental model that says cause and effect are usually disproportionately distributed. Something always worth keeping in mind. The lesson itself introduced the rule and then showed a few examples, I brushed over this one as I was already familiar with the concept.

Practice Problem Solving

I'll admit, I was getting tired at this point, the course had been going for almost an hour. I expected this section to be a wrap up of everything discussed so far, so I turned down the turbo mode and relaxed a bit. I looked at the list of principles, mental models, and tools that I'd got and watched the final section at about 30% effort, watching to see how and where these principles and models were applied, but I didn't add anything new.

The Result

Let's conclude. From a one-hour course on Problem Solving I extracted the following.

  • Principle: Evaluate the Root Cause. Solve causes not consequences. Causes are a tree, make sure you're considering the right ones. Break the problem into components. Use Five Whys as a tool, and keep in mind the Cause and Effect Web.

  • Principle: Consider the Consequences. Consider what new problems your fix will introduce. Use Seven So-Whats as a tool, and keep in mind the Cause and Effect Web.

  • Principle: Understand the Problem. Write a good problem statement. Consider why you're being asked to solve the problem. Consider Question Behind the Question. Use the Problem Statement tool to capture your output.

  • Principle: Don't Reinvent the Wheel. Review previous projects to see if this problem has occurred/been solved before. If it has, what is different now that wasn't the case back then.

  • Principle: Consider Different Points of View. Look at your problem from multiple angles to get a different perspective. Specifically, see it through the eyes of the CEO, or Sales, or Engineering. What happens if you relax an assumption or constraint, what changes?

  • Mental Model: Question Behind the Question Says that when someone asks for your help or asks you to solve a problem, they're not usually asking you to solve their problem, but instead asking you to solve what they think is a sub-problem that will solve their problem. If instead, you can find out what their true problem is, you might be able to sidestep the issue and help them solve their problem directly. You might know something they don't.

  • Mental Model: Cause and Effect Web. Says that the causes for and consequences of a particular problem are a messy and tangled web, it's probably not even directional, no doubt that there are feedback loops and cycles. The model says, don't trust or rely too much on a simplification, the reality is very complex, especially in organisations.

  • Tool: Five Whys A tool for exploring a causal chain of reasoning. Helps you dive deeper into the cause part of the Cause and Effect Tree. Is like a depth-first search. Be wary of not considering branches. Simply keep asking "Why". Five is arbitrary.

  • Tool: Seven So-Whats A tool for exploring an effect chain. Helps you explore the consequence part of the Cause and Effect Tree. Is like a depth-first search. Be wary of not considering branches. Simply keep asking "So What". Seven is arbitrary.

No doubt I'll have to adapt the process as I go, especially when those lists start getting very long, but it's working well for the time being.
