On Not Burning Out

11th January 2021

This is a short story of my personal experience. It is a reminder that balance in life is important.

I deliberately called this post On Not Burning Out, because I don't believe I have ever fully burned out. But I have come close several times, and recently it happened again. Despite the experience being a stressful one, I consider myself lucky, because it served as a stark reminder that I need to keep things in balance, and it's only now that I have the time to think about and appreciate the lesson, that I realise how important it really is. It's so important, that I'm writing this post as a reminder, mostly for future me, but for others as well. We could all do with such a reminder every now and again.

I, like a lot of people, like to be ambitious and sometimes bite off a bit more than I can chew. Last year was one of those times, and for the 9 months from March to December 2020, I slowly committed to more and more, and increased my involvement in those commitments. It's not important what those commitments are, but they weren't just commitments of my time and energy. They were commitments to taking responsibility for things, and those can be some of the most intensive commitments of all.

At the end of November, it was beginning to burn me out, and things were beginning to slip. The funny thing about committing is that when it's all going smoothly, you have the illusion of control, but when just one commitment starts to become volatile, you quickly realise how little capacity you have to absorb that volatility. One of my biggest commitments unexpectedly blew up in my face, and because I was so invested, I had no choice but to stick it out - at the expense of everything else. My mental health suffered, and my energy and enthusiasm took a dive.

Fortunately, the christmas break forced a natural pause in most (but not all) of my commitments. I was surprised by how long it took me to recover. I was surprised by how long it took me to recover, I didn't bounce back in the space of a few days, I went through several stages of recovery before I began feeling like myself again. I slowed the pace right down and it gave me time to think and reflect on what had happened.

Benefits. An opportunity to grow up. Simply slowing down and reflecting on my experience, I discovered something remarkable. I've noticed I've become more thoughtful, I have more ideas. I suspect it's because I'm giving them space to appear, more ideas benefits all my commitments, I am more creative. I found I had more patience for people, more compassion. I rediscovered that the purpose of my life is not simply to produce and achieve as much as I can, at some point I should probably enjoy myself along the way, otherwise what am I doing it all for? The funny thing is, when you're racing at a hundred miles an hour, you simply don't have time to remind yourself of such things, when those are precisely the things you need reminding of most. I can read books slowly, and dive more deeply into ideas that interest me.
