Seriously Active Reading

Introduction

What is this article all about and why should you read it? Active reading (in contrast with passive reading) is the process of trying to actively remember what you read by engaging with the material as you read it. More often than not that means taking notes, pausing regularly to reflect and connect the ideas with your other experiences, and following up with the information afterwards using techniques like spaced repetition. The advantage of doing this is obvious, you learn more and remember more of it!

Unfortunately though, active reading takes a lot of effort, and you simply can't apply it to everything you read, it'd be too exhausting. But there are certainly occasions where you do want to really absorb and assimilate the material, and it's nice to have this "turbo mode" that you can switch on and off as and when you need it.

This article is a tutorial in my system for extracting as much value as possible from the material, remembering it, and then applying it in my life. I wanted to share it here as I've been asked about it a few times and it's useful to have somewhere to direct people to. This system is a variation of other reading systems out there that I have personalised and made work for me, I hope you find some of the ideas useful. Here's a quick summary of how my system works, then I'll go through each part in more detail with an example case study.

In short, my system boils down to extracting mental models, principles, and tools. I add these to three growing lists respectively, and have adopted the habit of regularly reviewing those lists (which is my version of spaced repetition). That's it.

Mental Models

Principles

A principle is the term I give to the little rules of thumb that you end up collecting over your lifetime, the proverbial fatherly wisdom handed down through the generations. They are almost like rules, but tend to be rather more abstract and general. You can think of them simply as bits of general purpose advice. The goal of a principle is to provide guidance in decision making, particularly in deciding how to act and behave in various situations. It's easier to explain with a few examples.

Prioritise Foundational Skills. This is a principle for helping me decide what to learn, because there's far too much for learn for one lifetime. This principle reminds me that programming frameworks and languages come and go but that by mastering the underlying concepts and ideas beneath those frameworks is more enduring. No doubt you've heard similar advice before, this is my way of encoding and remembering it.

Affordable Loss Principle. This principle dictates that I find ways to accomplish what I want to do with the minimal possible expenditure of effort. Taking a product to market? Skip the market research and analysis, go try and sell the thing instead. I'll learn much more!

If You Don't Ask You Don't Get. I'm sure that I don't need to explain this one, but it's so true and I've found value in reminding myself of it regularly.

You can find lists and lists of these all over the internet, usually from articles named things like "50 Lessons for life", or "The Best Advice My Dad Ever Gave Me". Although these are good places to start, I've found it more effective to build my own list with those that are the most meaningful to me, it grows over time. The most valuable place I pick up new principles now is from reading blogs and articles from insightful writers.

You do have to be careful of the level at which you collect them, otherwise you may end up with far too many. For example, "Wake up at 7am" probably isn't a principle worth keeping in your list, however perhaps "Keep a consistent sleeping routine" is more appropriate, it depends on what's important and useful to you.
